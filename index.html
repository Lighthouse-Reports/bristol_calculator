Inside Bristol’s Think Family Database

The Think Family Database holds information on more than 54,000 families (about 170,000 people) across Bristol, without their consent. It’s a controversial tool that people know very little about – and we want to change that.

Every person on the database has a “profile” page that displays information about them from at least 35 different data sources held by Bristol City Council, Avon and Somerset Police, the NHS and national government. It pulls together information such as an individual's mental health records, homelessness status, records of arrests or police interaction, things like whether adults are enrolled in parenting courses, and details of teenage pregnancies.

The data is used to create a ‘risk score’ for children, which can be viewed by police, by their teachers at school, social workers and council staff. While the city council claims the database’s goal is to make it easier to identify the need for support for vulnerable families, critics worry that it’s a surveillance tool, not a safeguarding one – functioning to profile already marginalised families.

Investigative newsroom Lighthouse Reports, working with the Cable, has created a calculator to help people understand how this ‘risk scoring’ on the Think Family database works.
